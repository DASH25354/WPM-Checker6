【6】Their meeting was a chance event. In 1977, Mr. Shimon Sakaguchi began immunology research at Aichi Cancer Center. During a summer lab tour, Ms. Kyoko, a Nagoya City University medical student, visited. She saw he was serious and modest, but his intense eyes in experiments were unique. They grew closer and married. In 1990, they moved to the United States. She liked new places, learned lab skills, and worked as his assistant. They studied regulatory T cells when many scientists said such suppressor cells did not exist. Their team was small, but they kept strong faith. Back in Japan, they continued. In 2003, a Nature paper listed her as first author. Their work on regulatory T cells led to the Nobel Prize. In 2025, the Karolinska Institute announced the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine to Mr. Sakaguchi , a Kyoto University emeritus and Osaka University professor, and two others. yomiuri.co.jp Now she works at his startup; their lab has about 30 people, and she is the “lab mom.”
